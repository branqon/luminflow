Visual Overhaul — Three Approaches

Each approach delivers the full immersive vision, but differs in how far the UI dissolves into the canvas

A

Liquid Canvas — Recommended

The canvas IS the UI. Controls emerge as glowing glyphs and fluid shapes directly on the canvas surface. Mood, key, and scale appear as luminous text that breathes with the animation. The dock dissolves entirely — controls orbit the cursor or appear at edges when you drift toward them. Each mood transforms the fluid physics: Still Water = slow ripples and caustics, Moon Drift = swirling nebula ink, Warm Rain = falling droplets creating expanding rings.


Strengths

  • Most immersive — nothing breaks the illusion
  • Controls feel like part of the instrument
  • Strongest mood differentiation
  • Dramatic intro potential (fluid fills the screen)

Trade-offs

  • Highest complexity — fluid sim + contextual UI positioning
  • Discoverability risk — users may not find controls
  • Performance demands (WebGL/shader-based rendering)
B

Glass Dissolution

Glass cards that melt into the canvas. Keep the card-based UI concept but make cards translucent, borderless, and animated — they pulse with the music, blur into the background, and subtly shift position. The canvas gets the full fluid treatment. Controls slide in from edges on hover/gesture and fade out during play. Status info (mood, key) rendered as floating, ethereal labels that drift slowly. Each mood changes card tinting, blur intensity, and the fluid canvas beneath.


Strengths

  • Familiar card metaphor, but feels new
  • Easier control discoverability
  • Gradual transition from current design
  • Still very immersive during play

Trade-offs

  • Cards still feel like "UI on top of art" even if refined
  • Less radical transformation per mood
  • The dissolution effect can feel gimmicky if not tuned right
C

Theater Mode

Two distinct states: stage and backstage. During play, the screen is 100% fluid canvas — zero UI, total immersion. A deliberate gesture (long press, swipe up, or edge tap) transitions into a "backstage" view where controls appear in a cinematic overlay — a sleek panel that slides over the fluid like a curtain. The fluid keeps animating behind it, tinted and blurred. Each mood has its own backstage styling. Info like mood name and key appear as subtle watermarks on the canvas during play.


Strengths

  • Maximum visual impact during play — nothing in the way
  • Clean separation between "perform" and "configure"
  • Backstage panel can be richly designed
  • Simplest canvas rendering (no UI integration needed)

Trade-offs

  • Two-state model may feel less fluid/organic
  • Quick setting changes require mode switching
  • Less "persistent info" visible during play
